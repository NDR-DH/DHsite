Mediation

A dispute that needs to be resolved
within the reality of the system.

Mediation can offer a structured route to resolution in regulatory and institutional disputes. But the question is not simply whether mediation is available. It is whether the dispute is one that mediation can genuinely address.

In this context, resolution does not mean finding a compromise that feels acceptable in the room. It means reaching an outcome that reflects what the relevant framework allows, that each party has the legal authority to agree to, and that can actually be implemented once the mediation ends. An agreement that unravels on contact with the regulatory or statutory framework it was supposed to operate within is not a resolution.

The value of mediation here depends on whether those constraints are understood from the outset — and whether the issues in dispute are ones that a mediated process can realistically address. That assessment comes first.

What makes this kind of mediation different

Regulatory and institutional disputes are not generic commercial disputes. The parties are not simply two businesses with competing interests who need a structured conversation. One or both parties may be operating under legal, regulatory, or policy constraints that fundamentally shape what they can and cannot agree to.

A regulator or public body does not have unlimited freedom to negotiate. Its functions are defined by statute. Its powers are bounded. Its decisions must be consistent with its legal obligations and, in many cases, with published policy. A representative who engages constructively in a mediation session may still not be in a position to agree to what is being sought — not because of bad faith, but because the organisation they represent lacks the authority to do so.

Generic commercial mediation, conducted without an understanding of those constraints, risks producing agreements that cannot be honoured, narrowing issues in ways that are unhelpful, or allowing discussions to drift toward outcomes that are not within the system's scope to deliver. The success of mediation in this context depends on understanding those constraints clearly before and during the process — not discovering them afterwards.

What we bring to it

Understanding the operating framework

We come to these mediations with direct knowledge of the regulatory and institutional frameworks within which the parties are operating — what each party can lawfully agree to, where the limits of their authority lie, and what a resolution that is consistent with those frameworks looks like before the process begins. That knowledge is not acquired during the mediation. It shapes how the mediation is structured and what it is realistically trying to achieve.

Distinguishing what is negotiable from what is not

Not every aspect of a regulatory dispute is open to resolution through negotiation. Some issues are fixed — by statute, by policy, by the terms of a licence or approval — and no amount of goodwill on either side can change that. Others involve discretion that can legitimately be exercised differently, or a factual or procedural question that has not been properly resolved. Identifying that distinction precisely is often the difference between a mediation that makes progress and one that does not.

Keeping discussions grounded

We ensure that discussions remain anchored to what the framework permits and what each party can actually deliver. Where a proposed outcome is one that the regulatory body has no authority to agree to, or where an agreement would require a party to act inconsistently with its legal obligations, we identify that before it becomes the basis of an agreement. Discussions that feel constructive but produce outcomes that cannot be implemented are not productive.

Identifying where the real dispute lies

In regulatory disputes, the stated positions of the parties often obscure a more specific disagreement — about the legal basis for a decision, the exercise of a discretionary power, the interpretation of a policy, or the application of a standard. Locating that underlying question precisely is often what makes resolution possible. Where the real issue is a legal question that requires determination rather than a matter that can be negotiated, we say so.

When mediation is appropriate

The relationship between the parties must continue

Where the dispute is between an organisation and the body that regulates or commissions it, the relationship does not end when the dispute is resolved. The regulator will continue to regulate. The commissioner will continue to commission. A resolution that damages the ongoing relationship irremediably is rarely in either party's interest, and mediation that produces a workable outcome and preserves the basis for continued engagement often has value beyond the immediate dispute.

The dispute is real but not purely legal

Where the disagreement involves factual questions, questions about the exercise of discretion, or questions about the application of a standard — rather than a fundamental legal issue about whether a power was lawfully exercised — there is often room for a structured process to identify the real area of disagreement and work through it. These are the disputes most likely to benefit from mediation.

The issue involves communication, application, or discretion

Some regulatory disputes arise not from a clear legal error but from the way a framework has been applied in practice — inconsistently, without adequate explanation, or in a way that has not engaged with the relevant considerations. Where the issue is one of application rather than legal principle, there is often more room for resolution than the formal positions of the parties suggest.

Both parties can reach an agreement they have authority to honour

Mediation is only worth pursuing if there is a realistic prospect of an outcome that both parties can implement. That requires that each party has sufficient authority and flexibility to reach a resolution, and that the resolution will not be unpicked by constraints that were not addressed during the process. Establishing that before committing to mediation is part of the assessment we provide.

When mediation is not appropriate

The dispute turns on a point of law

Where the core issue is whether a decision was lawfully made — whether the decision-maker had the power to act as they did, whether the correct legal test was applied, whether the required process was followed — that question cannot be resolved through negotiation. It requires a legal opinion or, where the parties cannot agree, a judicial determination. Mediation in this context adds cost and delay without addressing the issue.

The decision requires formal determination

Some regulatory decisions have a formal appeal or review route — a statutory right of appeal, an internal review process, or a challenge in the courts — that is the appropriate mechanism for contesting them. Where that is the case, mediation may not be the right first step, or may need to be considered alongside rather than instead of those formal routes.

The position cannot lawfully be negotiated

Where the regulatory body's position reflects a statutory obligation, a binding policy, or a legal constraint that it has no power to depart from, mediation will not produce a different outcome however constructive the process. Identifying that constraint clearly, and advising on what follows from it, is more useful than proceeding as though the position were open to negotiation when it is not.

We are direct about this

Where mediation is unlikely to be productive, we say so — before the process begins, not after it has failed. That assessment is part of what we provide, and it is not a formality. In some cases the conclusion will be that the appropriate route is a formal challenge, a structured representations process, or further analysis before any next step is taken.

What the process looks like

Initial assessment of suitability

The first step is a focused assessment of whether mediation is appropriate for the dispute in question — the nature of the issues, the positions of the parties, what each can lawfully agree to, and whether there is a realistic prospect of an outcome that the framework permits and that both parties have authority to implement. This assessment is not a preliminary formality. It is substantive, and it may conclude that mediation is not the right route at this stage.

Clarification of the issues in dispute

Before approaching the other party, the issues in dispute need to be clearly identified — what is genuinely at issue, what is within the scope of a mediated process to address, and what, if anything, needs to be resolved through other means before mediation can usefully proceed. Getting that clarity in advance is often what allows the mediation itself to be productive.

Approach to the other party

Where mediation is appropriate, we manage the approach to the other party — framing the proposal clearly, agreeing the scope and structure of the process, and ensuring that both sides understand what the mediation is and is not intended to achieve. How a mediation is proposed affects whether it proceeds, and whether it is approached in a way that is likely to be productive.

The mediation

We conduct the mediation with a clear understanding of the regulatory and institutional framework within which the parties are operating. Discussions are kept grounded in what is legally achievable. Where proposed outcomes are ones that cannot be implemented within the framework, we identify that during the process rather than after it has concluded.

Outcome, narrowed issues, or clearer route forward

Where mediation produces an agreement, we ensure it is expressed precisely, that each party has the authority to honour it, and that it is consistent with the framework within which both parties are operating. Where it does not produce a full agreement, it may still clarify the issues in dispute, narrow what remains to be resolved, or identify more precisely what route — formal challenge, further engagement, or otherwise — is appropriate for what remains.

The relationship to guidance and dispute

Some matters begin at the guidance stage — where the situation is still being understood — and move into mediation once the issues are clear and a structured process is identified as the right route. The analysis done at the guidance stage is not wasted. It informs the assessment of whether mediation is suitable and, where it is, how the process should be structured.

Some disputes, once the issues have been clearly identified, become suitable for mediation. An initial formal position — a challenge, a set of representations, an exchange of correspondence — sometimes clarifies the issues sufficiently to make a structured process productive where it would not have been at an earlier stage.

Where mediation is not suitable — because the issue requires formal determination, or because the other party will not or cannot engage — the appropriate route may be a formal challenge or a structured dispute process. Dawson House supports those transitions. We do not conduct litigation ourselves, but we work alongside solicitors and counsel who specialise in the relevant area and ensure that the work done to that point is not lost in the transition.

The point is not agreement at any cost. It is finding out whether this dispute can be resolved in a way that is lawful, workable, and worth pursuing.
