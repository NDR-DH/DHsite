Professional deputy

A dispute involving your client.
A responsibility that is yours.

You are acting as a professional deputy for a person who lacks capacity. A disagreement has arisen — about care, funding, placement, treatment, best interests, or the exercise of your authority. Another party has taken a position: a local authority, an NHS body, a care provider, or a family member.

The issue is not simply that there is disagreement. It is whether the position taken is justified, lawfully reached, and properly grounded in the relevant framework. Those are distinct questions, and the answer to each shapes what can and should be done in response.

The task is the same as elsewhere: to understand what has happened, how the decision has been reached, and what can realistically be done about it.

What is actually happening

Decisions in these systems — social care commissioning, NHS continuing healthcare, mental capacity assessments, best interests determinations — are not made in a vacuum. They are shaped by process requirements, institutional pressures, internal guidance, and the exercise of discretion by individual decision-makers. What is communicated to you as a final position is often neither clearly explained nor fully justified against the framework it is supposed to reflect.

Reasoning is frequently incomplete. Legal obligations are described in terms of policy rather than statute. Decisions that engage the Mental Capacity Act or the Care Act are presented as administrative outcomes rather than as exercises of specific powers subject to specific constraints. And positions that appear fixed often have more give in them than the communication suggests — not because the institution is acting in bad faith, but because the gap between the framework and how it is applied in practice is where most of the real room for resolution lies.

Understanding which kind of problem you are dealing with — and where within the framework the dispute actually sits — is the necessary starting point.

How we approach these situations

Understanding the decision

We begin by establishing precisely what legal framework applies to the decision in question — whether that is the Mental Capacity Act, the Care Act, NHS continuing healthcare frameworks, or a combination — and what that framework required of the decision-maker. We then examine what process should have been followed, what actually happened, and whether the position that has been communicated to you is one that can be sustained on a proper application of the relevant law.

That analysis is often more illuminating than the decision itself. It identifies whether there is a substantive issue, a procedural failure, or both — and what the implications of each are for how the matter should be approached.

Identifying the real issue

Disputes in this area tend to arise from one of several distinct kinds of failure, and identifying which applies determines what is available in response. A legal error — where the wrong framework has been applied, or the statutory test has been misunderstood — carries different implications from a procedural failure, where the correct test may have been applied but without following the required process. A misuse of discretion — where the decision-maker had power to act but exercised it improperly or without considering relevant factors — is different again from a failure to give adequate weight to your client's own wishes and feelings, which is a specific and frequently recurring issue in best interests decisions.

And some disputes arise not from any of these, but from a breakdown in engagement — where the relationship between your client's interests and the institution responsible for serving them has deteriorated to the point where correspondence alone is unlikely to move things forward.

We identify which kind of problem is present before we advise on how to address it.

Mapping the options

Most disputes have a range of available responses, and the right one depends on the nature of the issue, the position of the other party, and where you are in the process. The options typically include: direct engagement with the decision-maker or a more senior person within the institution; structured written representations setting out the legal position clearly; use of formal complaint processes, internal review rights, or statutory mechanisms; mediation, where the relationship needs to be preserved and the issues are ones a structured process can address; and, where necessary, escalation to formal legal challenge.

We do not recommend a course of action until the analysis is complete and the available options are clearly understood. The appropriate response to one kind of problem is often unhelpful or counterproductive when applied to another.

Early resolution where possible

Many disputes involving professional deputies can be resolved without formal proceedings. A well-constructed letter that sets out the legal position precisely — identifying the specific obligations engaged, the specific failures, and the specific remedy sought — can shift a position that appeared fixed. Reaching the right decision-maker within an institution, at the right stage of the process, is often more effective than correspondence directed at whoever communicated the original decision.

Early resolution is not always the appropriate route. Some decisions are not open to informal challenge, either because the institution has taken a considered position it is not willing to revisit, or because the issue genuinely requires a formal determination. And pursuing informal resolution in those circumstances can delay a better outcome while the window for more effective action narrows.

Part of what we provide at this stage is a clear view of which kind of situation you are in — one where early engagement is likely to be productive, or one where it is not — so that the decision about how to proceed is made accurately rather than optimistically.

When escalation is required

Some disputes require a formal determination. Court of Protection proceedings may be necessary where the issue involves a decision about deprivation of liberty, where there is a fundamental disagreement about what is in your client's best interests that cannot be resolved otherwise, or where a legal question requires judicial determination rather than negotiated resolution.

The analytical and advisory work done to that point does not restart at escalation. The understanding of the framework, the issues, the other party's position, and the available arguments carries forward directly into the litigation. The record built through careful advisory work — documenting what was considered, what steps were taken, and why — becomes the foundation for proceedings rather than something that needs to be reconstructed from scratch.

Where escalation is required, we ensure continuity into litigation through closely aligned regulated practices, so that the matter proceeds without loss of understanding or direction.

The relationship to mediation

Some disputes involving professional deputies are well suited to mediation — particularly where the relationship between your client and the institution responsible for their care or funding needs to continue, and where the issues in dispute are ones that a structured process can genuinely address.

Mediation in this context is not a soft alternative to proper analysis. It is a structured process that works when both parties are capable of reaching an agreement they have legal authority to honour, and when the issues are ones that can be resolved by agreement rather than requiring judicial determination. Where those conditions are met, it can produce more durable outcomes than correspondence and avoid the damage to an ongoing relationship that formal proceedings often cause.

Where they are not met — because the issue turns on a legal question, because the other party cannot agree to what is being sought, or because the relationship has deteriorated beyond the point where a structured process is likely to be productive — we say so clearly.
