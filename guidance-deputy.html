Professional deputy

Guidance in decisions affecting your client.
Before you decide what to do next.

You are acting as a professional deputy for a person who lacks capacity. A situation has arisen — a decision about care, funding, treatment or placement — and something about it is unclear, incomplete, or potentially wrong.

You may not yet be in a dispute. You may not yet know whether one exists. What is needed at this stage is not a plan for escalation. It is clarity — about what the relevant framework requires, what has actually happened, and what your realistic options are before any further step is taken.

This is where early advisory input is most useful. And where the decisions made most consequentially shape what follows.

When guidance is needed

Uncertainty about the applicable framework

The systems you are operating within — local authority social care, NHS continuing healthcare, Mental Capacity Act decision-making — are complex and not always transparent about how they function. Decisions are often communicated without clear reference to the legal framework that governs them. When you are not certain what framework applies, or what it actually requires, that uncertainty needs to be resolved before you act.

Unclear legality of a decision or process

A decision has been made — about your client's care, placement, funding, or treatment — and something about it does not correspond with what the law requires. The process may not have been followed correctly. The statutory criteria may not have been met. Before you decide whether to challenge it, you need a clear account of what went wrong and whether it is something that can and should be addressed.

Emerging disagreement

A relationship with a local authority, an NHS body, a care provider, or members of the family is becoming difficult. Nothing has hardened into a formal dispute yet. But the direction of travel is concerning, and you need to understand what your position is and what, if anything, you should be doing now rather than waiting.

Best interests and proportionality questions

You are facing a decision — about whether to pursue a particular step, how to respond to a proposal, or how to weigh competing considerations — and you need an independent assessment of what your client's best interests require and whether the action you are considering is proportionate to what is at stake.

Whether and how to escalate

Something has happened and you are not sure whether it warrants a formal response. The decision about whether to escalate — and how — is itself a professional judgment that needs to be made carefully. Advice at this stage does not commit you to anything. It gives you the basis on which to make that judgment properly.

What we do

Identify the relevant framework

We establish what legal and regulatory framework the situation engages — the Mental Capacity Act, the Care Act, NHS continuing healthcare frameworks, or a combination — and what that framework requires of the institution involved and of you as deputy. This is often the clearest starting point, because it identifies precisely what should have happened and what actually did.

Analyse what has happened

We examine what the institution has done, what it has not done, whether the correct process has been followed, and whether the decision or position communicated to you is consistent with what the law requires. That analysis is often more informative than the decision itself — it locates whether there is a substantive issue, a procedural one, or both.

Map the available options

Most situations have a range of possible responses: informal clarification, structured representations, formal complaint, statutory review, mediation, pre-action correspondence, and — where necessary — legal challenge. We set out what each involves, what it is likely to achieve, and how it fits within your obligations as deputy. We do not recommend a course of action until that map is complete.

Advise on proportionate next steps

Not every situation requires escalation. Not every concern justifies the cost and disruption of a formal process. We give a direct view on what is proportionate given what is actually at stake — including when the right response is to do nothing further, and when it is not. We are equally precise about genuine urgency and about situations where there is time to think carefully before acting.

What an initial instruction looks like

Advisory work at this stage is structured as a defined piece of work rather than an open-ended engagement. The scope is agreed before we begin, and you are not committed to any further step until you have decided to take it.

Written assessment

The most common starting point is a written assessment of the situation: what has happened, what framework applies, what the relevant obligations are, and what the available options look like. That assessment gives you something concrete to act on — and a documented basis for the decisions you take, which matters if those decisions are later examined.

Focused and bounded

Each piece of work is defined by a specific question and a clear scope. We identify what we are being asked to address, we address it, and we set out clearly what the next decision point is. You are not drawn into a process before you have decided whether you want to pursue one.

Practical and decision-focused

Our advice is written to be used. It identifies the legal position, the realistic options, and what each would involve — so that the decision about how to proceed can be made on an accurate understanding of the situation rather than a theoretical one.

Why this matters

Supports defensible decision-making

The Office of the Public Guardian and the Court of Protection assess deputy decision-making by reference to reasonableness and proportionality. A clear record of what was considered, what advice was obtained, and on what basis a course of action was chosen or declined strengthens your position if those decisions are later reviewed. Advisory input that is properly documented is not just substantively valuable — it is evidentially valuable.

Avoids unnecessary escalation

Many situations that reach formal dispute — and some that reach court — would have resolved differently if the legal position had been clearly understood at an earlier stage. Early advice does not always prevent escalation. But it ensures that when escalation occurs, it is chosen rather than arrived at by default, and that the steps taken before it are ones that can be explained and justified.

Creates a clear record

Guidance obtained before a dispute has formed becomes part of the record of the matter. Where further steps are later required, it demonstrates that decisions were considered, proportionate, and preceded by proper analysis. That record is useful in dealings with the OPG, in court proceedings, and in any subsequent review of how the matter was handled.

Protects your client and your position

Acting without a clear understanding of the framework creates risk — for your client, whose interests may not be advanced by the step taken, and for you, whose decision-making may be difficult to justify in retrospect. Early clarity reduces that risk on both sides.

The relationship to mediation and dispute

Guidance at this stage is not the same as managing a dispute. It is the stage before that — where the situation is still being understood and the options have not yet narrowed.

Some situations, once properly understood, resolve without any further formal step. A well-constructed letter, a structured conversation with the right person, or a clear statement of the legal position can shift a decision that appeared fixed.

Others move toward mediation — where the disagreement is real but the relationship needs to be preserved and the issues can be worked through in a structured process. Others require formal challenge. The guidance stage does not determine which of those applies. It identifies it.

Where the situation does move to mediation or dispute, the analysis and documentation produced at the guidance stage does not fall away. It becomes the foundation for what follows.

You need to understand what you are dealing with.
