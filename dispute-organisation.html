Dispute

A regulatory decision.
A position that does not hold.

A decision has been made — an inspection finding, an enforcement action, a requirement, a refusal, a sanction. The reasoning given does not correspond with what the framework actually requires. The process may not have been followed. The discretion exercised may not have been lawfully exercised. Or the decision simply does not hold up against what the relevant obligations actually say.

This is not only a matter of disagreement. It is a question of whether the decision is properly grounded — legally, procedurally, and factually. Before you decide how to respond, that question needs a precise answer.

Acting without that answer — whether by accepting a position that should not be accepted, or by challenging one in ways that are unlikely to succeed — is rarely in your interests. The starting point is understanding what has actually happened and what follows from it.

What is actually happening

Regulatory decisions are not made in a vacuum. They are shaped by institutional pressures, internal guidance, resource constraints, and the discretion of individual decision-makers — factors that are rarely visible from the outside and are not reflected in the formal framework.

What appears to be a fixed and final position is often neither. Regulatory bodies exercise judgment, and where that judgment is exercised incorrectly — where the wrong framework has been applied, where the process has not been followed, where the facts have been mischaracterised — the decision can be questioned. But knowing whether that is the case, and where the question lies, requires a detailed understanding of how the relevant system actually works, not just what it says on paper.

The gap between the formal framework and how it is applied is precisely where disputes in this area arise. Understanding that gap is what makes it possible to respond effectively — to identify what is actually at issue, what is worth pursuing, and how.

How we approach these situations

Understanding the decision

We begin by identifying what framework applies to the decision in question — what it requires, what process should have been followed, and what the decision-maker was and was not entitled to do. We then examine what actually happened: what reasoning was given, whether that reasoning is consistent with the framework, and whether the decision is one that can be sustained.

That analysis is often more informative than the decision itself. It identifies whether there is a substantive issue, a procedural one, or both — and what the implications of each are.

Identifying the real issue

Not all disputes arise from the same kind of failure. Some involve a clear legal error — where the decision-maker has misapplied the relevant framework or acted outside the scope of their authority. Some involve a procedural failure — where the correct process was not followed, or where the affected party was not given the opportunity to which they were entitled. Some involve a misuse of discretion — where the decision was within the decision-maker's power but was reached on an improper basis. And some arise from a breakdown in engagement — where the relationship has deteriorated to the point where formal challenge has become the only available route.

Identifying which kind of problem you are dealing with is not a formality. It determines what responses are available, which are likely to succeed, and how the matter should be framed.

Mapping the options

Most disputes have a range of possible responses: informal engagement with the decision-maker, structured representations, a formal complaint or internal review, mediation, or formal legal challenge. Each carries different implications — for the outcome, for the relationship, for time and cost, and for what happens next if it does not succeed.

We set out what each option involves, what it is realistically likely to achieve, and how they relate to each other. We do not recommend a course of action until that map is complete and you have a clear view of the position you are actually in.

Early resolution where possible

Many regulatory disputes can be resolved without formal escalation. A well-constructed set of representations, precisely framed around what the framework requires and where the decision falls short of it, can shift a position that appeared fixed. Engaging with the right person in the right institution, at the right stage, is often more effective than a formal complaint or legal challenge — faster, cheaper, and less likely to damage the relationship permanently.

That is not always the appropriate route. Some decisions are not open to informal resolution, either because the institution is unwilling to engage or because the issue requires a formal determination. Where that is the case, early resolution is not a realistic option and should not be pursued as though it were.

The value of understanding the decision properly at the outset is that it allows you to identify which kind of situation you are in — one where early engagement is the right approach, or one where it is not — before committing to a course of action that may not serve your interests.

When escalation is required

Some decisions require a formal response. That may involve using an internal review or appeal process within the regulatory body, making a formal complaint to an oversight body, or commencing legal proceedings to challenge the decision through the courts.

Where escalation is necessary, what matters is that it is approached with a clear understanding of the legal basis for the challenge, the strength of the position, the likely response of the other side, and the realistic range of outcomes. Escalating without that understanding — or escalating on a basis that cannot be sustained — rarely produces the result sought and often makes the situation more difficult.

Dawson House supports preparation and strategy at the escalation stage: advising on the grounds for challenge, the framing of the case, and how to approach the process. We do not conduct litigation ourselves. Where formal legal proceedings become necessary, we work alongside solicitors and counsel who specialise in the relevant area, and we ensure that the analytical and advisory work done to that point translates effectively into the foundation those proceedings require.

The relationship to mediation

Some regulatory disputes are suited to mediation — particularly where the relationship between the parties needs to continue, where the issues are ones that can be worked through in a structured process, and where both sides have the authority and the willingness to reach a binding resolution.

Mediation in a regulatory context is not the same as mediation in a civil or commercial dispute. Regulatory bodies operate within legal frameworks that constrain what they can agree to. A mediated outcome that requires a regulatory body to act beyond its powers, or to agree to something that conflicts with its statutory obligations, will not hold. The value of mediation here depends on whether the mediator understands those constraints — and whether the issues in dispute are ones that a mediated process can actually address.

Where mediation is appropriate, we can advise on whether it is likely to be productive, how it should be structured, and what a realistic outcome looks like. Where it is not appropriate — because the issue turns on a point of law, or because the regulatory body cannot agree to what is being sought — we will say so clearly.
