Your situation

A dispute involving your client.
A responsibility that is yours.

You are a professional deputy acting for a person who lacks capacity. A dispute has arisen — with a local authority, an NHS body, a care provider, or within the family — and you are responsible for deciding how it should be addressed.

That is not only a practical question. It is a professional one.

The Office of the Public Guardian expects deputies to act in their client's best interests, to take proportionate steps, and to be able to demonstrate that the approach taken was reasoned and appropriate in the circumstances. Courts expect the same.

How a dispute is handled — and how that handling is documented — is part of careful professional decision-making. The choice to seek early advice, to consider mediation, or to move toward formal proceedings reflects the kind of reasoned, considered approach that good deputyship requires.

Where we support deputies

Disputes with local authorities

Disagreements over care packages, funding assessments, or the application of eligibility thresholds are among the most common disputes deputies encounter. Local authorities operate within constrained budgets and apply considerable discretion in how statutory duties are discharged. Understanding where that discretion has been exercised unlawfully or unreasonably — and how to challenge it effectively — requires working knowledge of how these systems function in practice, not only what the Care Act requires in principle.

Disputes with NHS bodies

NHS continuing healthcare decisions, treatment disputes, and disagreements about the scope of clinical responsibility engage frameworks that are technically complex and frequently misapplied. Where your client's interests are not being adequately considered within those processes, early and precise engagement is usually more effective than escalation.

Family conflict around best interests

Where family members hold different views about what is in your client's best interests — about where they should live, what care they should receive, or how their wishes should be understood — the deputy's position can become difficult to hold. The legal framework is clear about where authority lies, but disputes of this kind rarely resolve through authority alone. A structured process, properly managed, often produces more durable outcomes than correspondence.

Situations approaching the Court of Protection

Not every dispute requires a court application. But some do, and knowing the difference — and being able to demonstrate that alternatives were properly considered before proceedings were commenced — matters both for your client and for how your decision-making will be assessed. We help deputies understand where they are on that spectrum before they commit to a course of action.

How we work

Early advisory input

In many cases, what is most useful is a precise assessment of the situation before a position has hardened: what the relevant framework requires, what the institution involved has and has not done, and what the realistic range of responses looks like.

That analysis shapes everything that follows. It allows deputies to act from a clear understanding of the position rather than in reaction to it, and it produces a documented basis for the decisions taken — which matters if those decisions are later scrutinised.

Mediation

Mediation is one possible route. In the right case, it can resolve a dispute more quickly, with less cost, and in a way that preserves the relationships your client depends on — a local authority or NHS body that has been through litigation will usually remain responsible for your client's care or funding regardless of outcome.

But mediation is not always appropriate. Some disputes turn on points of law that require judicial determination. Some involve parties whose position is incompatible with their legal obligations. Proceeding to mediation in those circumstances adds cost without advancing the matter. We are direct about when it is the right route and when it is not — and where it is, we bring the framework knowledge that keeps the process grounded in what is actually achievable.

Transition to litigation

Where formal proceedings become necessary, the advisory and mediation work already done does not fall away. It forms the foundation for what follows: a clear record of the issues, the steps taken, and the basis on which litigation was judged to be required.

We do not conduct litigation ourselves, but we work closely with solicitors and counsel who specialise in Court of Protection proceedings and public law, and we support deputies through that transition in a way that keeps the matter clearly framed and properly documented.

Why this matters

Regulatory scrutiny

The decisions professional deputies make are subject to oversight by the Office of the Public Guardian and, where proceedings arise, by the Court of Protection. The standard against which those decisions are assessed is not simply whether the outcome was good, but whether the approach was reasoned, proportionate, and consistent with the deputy's obligations to act in the client's best interests.

That standard applies to the decision to pursue a dispute, to the manner in which it is conducted, and to the decision to settle or stand down. Deputies who can demonstrate careful, documented decision-making at each stage are better placed when those decisions are examined.

Evidencing the decision-making process

Early advisory input, a properly conducted mediation, and a clear record of why each step was taken are not only substantively valuable — they are evidentially valuable. Courts and the OPG are more likely to accept that a deputy acted appropriately where that decision-making is visible and traceable.

We structure our work with that in mind. The advice we give is clear about what it is based on. The process we recommend is one we can explain and, where necessary, account for.

Avoiding unnecessary escalation

Litigation is expensive, slow, and — in disputes that ultimately concern an ongoing care relationship — often damages the working relationship between your client and the bodies responsible for their care. Where a dispute can be resolved without proceedings, that is usually better for your client. Where it cannot, it is better to know that clearly and early than to arrive at the same conclusion after a prolonged and costly process.

Our aim is to help deputies reach that clarity as efficiently as possible — and to act on it in a way that is defensible, proportionate, and in their client's best interests.

The question is not simply what is available.
It is what is appropriate — and how it should be approached.
