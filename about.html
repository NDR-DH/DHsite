About

A considered approach
to complex decisions.

Dawson House is a specialist advisory and mediation practice. We work in mental capacity, health and social care, and disputes involving public bodies — areas where the legal and institutional frameworks are genuinely complex and the consequences of decisions are significant.

Our work is structured around clarity, careful analysis, and proportionate action. We help clients understand what they are dealing with before decisions are made, and we move through advisory, mediation and litigation support as the situation requires.

Why this work exists

The systems that govern health and social care, mental capacity and public authority decision-making are not designed to be legible to the people they affect. Decisions arrive without adequate explanation. Processes unfold without being visible. People are expected to respond to outcomes they do not fully understand, through routes they cannot easily identify, within timeframes they are not told about.

The result is that many situations that could be addressed — where something has gone wrong, where a decision does not reflect what the law requires, where a right exists but is not being exercised — are not addressed. Not because nothing can be done, but because knowing what can be done requires a kind of institutional knowledge that is not readily available.

Early understanding changes outcomes. A clear account of the legal position, the available options and the likely consequences of each — obtained before a dispute has hardened into a formal process — produces better results than the same analysis sought under pressure, after positions have been taken and routes have closed.

This is where Dawson House operates. Not as a general legal practice, but as a specialist resource for people navigating systems where that kind of knowledge matters and is not easily found elsewhere.

How we work

We begin with understanding the situation. That means identifying what has actually happened, what legal and institutional framework applies, and what the relevant decision-makers have and have not done. That analysis is the foundation of everything that follows — it is what allows us to give advice that is accurate rather than merely plausible.

From that starting point, we map the options that are genuinely available in the specific circumstances — not a theoretical account of what the law permits in the abstract, but a practical assessment of what is realistic given the institutions involved, the decisions that have been made, and the position the client is in now. We do not recommend a course of action until that map is complete.

We use mediation where it is appropriate. That means where the dispute involves an ongoing relationship, where the issues are ones that can be worked through with proper legal framing, and where both parties are capable of reaching an agreement they actually have authority to honour. Where mediation is not appropriate — because the dispute turns on a point of law, because the other party cannot agree to what is being sought, or because court determination is simply required — we say so and support the appropriate next step.

Where formal proceedings become necessary, we support the transition to litigation in a way that keeps the matter clearly framed and properly documented. We do not conduct litigation ourselves. We work alongside solicitors and counsel who specialise in Court of Protection proceedings and public law, and we ensure that the advisory and mediation work done translates effectively into the foundation those proceedings require.

Who we work with

Individuals, families and carers

People navigating decisions about care, funding, treatment or capacity — often at a point of significant stress, facing institutions that can appear impenetrable and processes that are not designed to be transparent. We help them understand what has happened, what they are entitled to, and what can realistically be done about it.

Professional deputies

Deputies acting for persons who lack capacity, managing disputes with local authorities, NHS bodies and care providers, or navigating situations that may require Court of Protection involvement. We work with deputies as a distinct client group, with an understanding of the regulatory obligations that shape how they approach decisions and how those decisions will later be assessed.

The situations we work in

Our work concentrates in disputes and decisions involving local authorities — particularly social care commissioning, funding assessments and eligibility determinations — NHS bodies, including continuing healthcare and treatment decisions, care providers, and proceedings in or approaching the Court of Protection. These are the systems where our institutional knowledge is deepest and where the gap between the formal legal position and how decisions are actually made is most consequential.

About the practitioner

Dawson House was founded by Necia Reith. Necia has practised for many years in Court of Protection proceedings and community care, working on disputes involving public bodies, complex best interests decisions, and the legal frameworks that govern health and social care. She is an accredited mediator with direct experience of mediation in disputes involving the Mental Capacity Act, statutory care obligations, and the institutional pressures that shape how those disputes arise and how they can be resolved.

The practice reflects a conviction that careful, specialist work — work that takes seriously both the legal framework and the human situation it governs — produces better outcomes than volume practice in complex areas. The structure of Dawson House is designed to support that kind of work: focused, rigorous, and accountable.

Working alongside regulated legal services

Dawson House is not a firm of solicitors. Our work encompasses advisory, mediation and consultancy services — analysis of legal frameworks, assessment of options, structured dispute resolution, and support for decision-making in complex institutional contexts.

Where a matter requires regulated legal representation — the conduct of litigation, the provision of legal aid services, or other activities reserved to authorised persons — that is identified clearly and appropriate referrals are made. We maintain working relationships with solicitors and counsel who specialise in the areas in which we operate, and we support the transition to regulated representation in a way that preserves continuity and keeps the matter clearly framed.

Clients who come to us at an early stage and subsequently require litigation support will not find themselves starting again. The analysis and documentation produced through our advisory or mediation work becomes the foundation for what follows.

You do not need a fully formed case.
You need to understand what you are dealing with.

Most people who contact us are not yet in formal proceedings. They are trying to understand a situation — what has happened, what it means, and whether anything can be done. That is the right time to ask.
